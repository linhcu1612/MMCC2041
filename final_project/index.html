Chess: Human vs. Computer

Origins of Chess

Chess has a vast history that stretches back many years, decades, and even centuries! But who invented the game that we all love? What was the genesis of this well-known mental practice that millions of people participated in?

Chess is well-known and widespread worldwide, yet its foundations and origins are unclear and widely contested. Several tales, anecdotes, and informed guesses range from where chess originated to when it was developed.

However, most people think that a single person did not form the established game since it is simply too complicated with all its rules and principles for any fundamental human mind to produce. One of those older games was chaturanga, a Sanskrit term for a military formation found in the Indian epic Mahabharata. Chaturanga flourished in northern India by the 7th century. It is the earliest forerunner of modern chess since it had two crucial elements with all later chess variants: various pieces had varying strengths, and success was centred on one piece, the contemporary chess king.

Over the next several decades, the game expanded throughout Asia and Europe, finally evolving into what we now call chess around the 16th century. 

The chess game was constantly upheaval until Wilhelm Steinitz became the first declared World Chess Champion in 1886. When we consider today's chess, we will struggle to recognise it: from a game driven by intuitive decision-making to a battle between chess computers.

Chess development over the time

Chess underwent several changes and withstood penalties by the Christian Church as well as total prohibitions from time to time until the end of the 15th century.

Chess grew in popularity over the world, and chess sets were standardised in the mid-nineteenth century. Prior to the 1850s, chess sets were not at all standardised. In 1849, Jaques of London (a toy and game company) produced a new kind of pieces designed by Nathaniel Cooke. These same pieces were endorsed by Howard Staunton, the strongest player of his time. This unique style of piece, known as the Staunton pattern, became instantly popular and was used in tournaments and clubs worldwide. The Staunton pieces, and minor variations, are still considered the standard for tournament chess sets. 

Until the 1880s, the game evolved into the modern, regarded as the "romantic chess age." Chess gradually developed a competitive aspect and specific rules, gaining appeal among the general public. Strategic planning and in-depth study of the theory were minor themes. There were concerns in the foreground, such as fast tactical themes and reliance on intuitive manoeuvres.

The first chess competitions were held, and in 1886, the first official World Championship was held, with Wilhelm Steinitz becoming the first recognised World Chess Champion.

There were romantic chess trends until the middle of the twentieth century when players depended on tactics and particularly dynamic play. However, the introduction of databases, chess engines, and many ways for pleasant and efficient strategical preparations in the twentieth century revolutionised chess.

Chess websites and online games were created to bring the romantic age to a close and usher in a new technological, digitalised era.

Human-computer chess matches until the 90s

The human-computer chess competition began in the mid-twentieth century. In 1949, Claude Shannon, widely regarded as the pioneer of modern IT, wrote a research article titled "Programming a Computer For Playing Chess" He described the first-ever chess engine algorithm in history in the paper.

Many computer engines were built on Shannon's work, although they were nowhere near as powerful as computers today. Until the 1970s, computers could barely compete with amateurs and could not compete with expert players.

Chess masters repeatedly harassed computers at the time. In 1968, IM David Levy wagered that a chess computer engine would not be able to defeat him for the following ten years. In 1978, he successfully cashed in 3000 US dollars since computers could not compete with human pattern recognition and intuition.

The voyage to teach computers to play chess began in the 1980s, when personal computers became widely available. As demand for chess software grew, businesses began to invest in its development. Soon after, computer-only competitions were created, with prizes granted to the first computer to reach the master level and defeat the world champion.

Unfortunately for computer scientists, during the 1980s, Kasparov won every game against chess computer engines.

Deep Blue and its battle against world champion

In the early 1990s, a group of IBM computer scientists created Deep Blue, a chess engine capable of exploring 200 million chess positions per second.

Deep Blue vs. Kasparov was the first match in 1996. Kasparov won the war for humanity once more, as he did in the 1980s, with a score of 4 to 2. That was the last occasion a human defeated a machine in a chess game. Soon afterwards, IBM planned a rematch for the next year (1997). Deep Blue was more prepared this time, with a more powerful CPU and a more vigorous opening repertoire. The encounter was conducted in New York's Equitable Center so that everyone may witness the outcome of a struggle that began 50 years before the first chess engine was developed. Kasparov won the first game using the KID (King's Indian Defense), leading IBM computer experts to mistrust Deep Blue's abilities. Current engines struggle with "understanding" some KID situations where experienced players might develop miraculous combinations to assault the enemy's king.

Fortunately for Deep Blue and IBM, Kasparov made a massive blunder after the second game and lost. Historians think this was the tipping point that shifted the balance of power in favour of our favourite chess engines. The following three games (3-5) were drawn out as Kasparov attempted to battle back but could not convert.

The contest quickly progressed to game six (and the last). In the Caro-Kann Defense, Kasparov used a questionable version. Perhaps Deep Blue assumed Deep Blue would not make the necessary knight sacrifice to get the advantage as White. Deep Blue's opening tree proved Kasparov's proved wrong, which crushed him in 18 moves.

Is The Game of Chess Solved?

Despite the superior power of chess engines and AlphaZero's innovative artificial intelligence-based chess, chess remains far from being solved. For chess to be solved permanently, every position must be identified, evaluated, and played in practice.

Scientists relate the number of potential chess positions to the number of atoms in the cosmos. To solve chess, you must be able to compute far more chess positions than present computers, and we, sadly (or not), do not yet have a quantum computer.

Furthermore, even if we solve it and record everything for future generations to study, it may be hard for the human brain to recall and absorb such a large quantity of knowledge.
