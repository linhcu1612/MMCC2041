Chess: Human vs. Computer

Origins of Chess

Many decades, centuries, and even millennia have passed since chess was invented! The question is, however, who invented this game that we all enjoy? During the past century, millions of people have engaged in this well-known practice of thinking. What was the origin of this practice?

There is no doubt that chess is well known and popular throughout the world, but its origins and foundations are unclear and widely contested. From the origins of chess to its development, there are numerous stories, anecdotes, and educated guesses.

People, however, are generally of the opinion that the established game could not have been created by a single individual. This is because it is too complex with all its rules and principles for any fundamental human mind to create it. The Indian epic Mahabharata described a military formation called chaturanga as one of those older games. As early as the 7th century, chaturanga flourished in northern India. Chess was the earliest example of modern chess because it shared two fundamental characteristics with the later forms of the game: various pieces had differing strengths, and success was mainly determined by one piece, the chess king of the day.

In the following decades, the game spread throughout Asia and Europe, eventually evolving into what we know today as chess. 

Until Wilhelm Steinitz became the first World Chess Champion in 1886, the chess game was constantly changing. It is difficult to recognize chess as it is played today: from a game driven by intuitive decision-making to a battle between chess computers driven by AI.

Chess development over the time

Throughout the period from the 15th century until the present day, chess has undergone several changes and resisted penalties imposed by the Christian Church as well as total prohibitions.

A chess set was standardised in the mid-nineteenth century as chess grew in popularity throughout the world. Prior to the 1850s, there was no standardization of chess sets. A revolutionary type of playing piece was designed by Nathaniel Cooke for Jaques of London (a toy and game company) in 1849. In his time, Howard Staunton was regarded as one of the strongest players in the world. As a result of this unique design, which was known as the Staunton pattern, the Staunton pattern became instantly popular. It was used in tournaments and clubs around the globe. As far as tournament chess sets are concerned, Staunton pieces, and minor variations, remain the standard. 

It took the modern game of chess until the 1880s to evolve into what is now considered to be the "romantic chess age." As chess developed a competitive aspect and specific rules, it gained popularity among the general public. It was also critical to emphasize strategic planning and to examine the theory in-depth. The importance of intuitive maneuvers and fast tactical themes was considered paramount.

As early as 1886, the first chess competitions took place, with Wilhelm Steinitz becoming the first recognized World Chess Champion.

Players relied on tactics and particularly dynamic play until the middle of the twentieth century when romantic chess trends began to emerge. In the twentieth century, however, the introduction of databases and chess engines revolutionized chess.

As the romantic era came to a close and a new technological, digital era dawned, chess websites and online games were created. These websites helped usher in this new era of technology and digitalization.

Human-computer chess matches until the 90s

In the mid-twentieth century, human-computer chess competitions were established. The first chess engine algorithm in history was described in 1948 in a research article written by Claude Shannon, widely regarded as the father of modern information technology.

Shannon's work was the foundation for many computer engines, though they were not nearly as powerful as computers today. Computers were barely competitive with amateurs and could not compete with experts before the 1970s.

Computers were repeatedly harassed by chess masters at that time. In 1968, IM David Levy wagered that he would never be defeated by a chess computer engine for the next ten years. As a result of the inability of computers to compete with human pattern recognition and intuition, he was successful in cashing in 3000 US dollars in 1978.

When personal computers became widely available in the 1980s, the journey to teach computers to play chess began. Companies began investing in the development of chess software as the demand for it grew. There was a short period of time before computer-only competitions became popular, with the first computer to defeat the world champion and achieve the master level receiving a prize.

Kasparov won every game against chess computers during the 1980s, which was unfortunate for computer scientists.

Deep Blue and its battle against world champion

During the early 1990s, IBM computer scientists created a chess engine known as Deep Blue, which is capable of exploring 200 million positions per second.

1996 marked the first match between Deep Blue and Kasparov. As in the 1980s, Kasparov won by a score of 4 to 2. This was the last time a human defeated a computer in a chess match. Several months later, IBM scheduled a rematch for the following year (1997). As a result of Deep Blue's stronger CPU and an expanded repertoire of opening moves, he was better prepared this time. This encounter took place at the Equitable Center in New York. This was in order to allow everyone to see the outcome of a struggle that began over 50 years before chess engines were developed. As a result of Kazarov's first victory using the KID (King's Indian Defense), IBM computer experts became distrustful of the capabilities of Deep Blue. A current engine has difficulty "understanding" some KID situations where experienced players are capable of developing miracle combinations in order to attack the enemy's king in an effective manner.

After the second game, Kasparov made a major blunder and lost to Deep Blue and IBM. It is believed by historians that this shifted the balance of power in favor of our favorite chess engines at this point. It took Kasparov three games (3-5) to gain a foothold in the match as he sought to battle back, but he was unable to accomplish this.

It was not long before game six (and the last) was played. A questionable version of the Caro-Kann Defense was used by Kasparov. There is a possibility that Deep Blue assumed that Deep Blue would not make the knight sacrifice necessary in order to gain an advantage as White. Kasparov was defeated in 18 moves by Deep Blue's opening tree.

Is The Game of Chess Solved?

Although chess engines are highly efficient and AlphaZero's artificial intelligence-based chess is innovative, chess remains far from being solved. It is necessary to identify, evaluate, and practice every position in order to solve chess permanently.

Chess positions can be compared to the number of atoms in the universe, according to scientists. There are a large number of chess positions that must be computed to solve chess. Present computers are unable to do this, and we do not possess a quantum computer at this point in time.

It may also be difficult for the human brain to retain and absorb such a large amount of information. This is even if we solve it and record everything to be studied by future generations.
